Deepfake Detection: Improving RECCE
Generalization Across Compression Levels

Problem Statement

Deepfake detectors are typically trained on high-quality video, but real-world video is almost always compressed. The RECCE model (Reconstruction-guided Classification for Effective Deepfake Detection, CVPR 2022) achieves 97% accuracy on lightly compressed FaceForensics++ video (C23) but drops to 74% on the same content under heavy compression (C40), creating a 23-point generalization gap. This gap is the core problem: a detector that fails under compression cannot be reliably deployed in the real world.

The goal of this project is to close that gap by testing three architectural modifications to RECCE: (1) a Frequency Branch that injects FFT magnitude spectrum features into the detector, (2) CBAM attention modules that direct the Xception encoder to focus on manipulation-relevant regions, and (3) Multi-Task heads that add auxiliary supervision for manipulation-type classification and mask prediction. We evaluate all combinations on FaceForensics++ at both compression levels and measure the generalization gap (C23 accuracy − C40 accuracy).

Dataset

We use FaceForensics++ (FF++), the standard benchmark for deepfake detection. It contains 1,000 original YouTube videos and their manipulated counterparts produced by four methods: DeepFakes, Face2Face, FaceSwap, and NeuralTextures. Each video is released at two compression levels:

  • C23 (light compression): high visual quality; manipulation artifacts are well-preserved and detectable. This is the training distribution.
  • C40 (heavy compression): strong JPEG-like compression destroys most manipulation artifacts, making detection far harder. This is the out-of-distribution test used to measure generalization.

All models are trained exclusively on C23 and evaluated on both C23 and C40 without retraining. We sample 16 frames per video for all experiments. The generalization gap (C23 accuracy − C40 accuracy) is our primary robustness metric; a lower gap means less degradation under compression.


Baseline model on C23 (light compression): 97% accuracy with only 557 missed fakes out of ~11,000.

The model receives a face crop from a video frame and outputs a binary prediction: Real or Fake. Predictions from 16 frames are aggregated to produce a final video-level decision. Original Model from Literature

RECCE (Cao et al., CVPR 2022) is a reconstruction-guided deepfake detector. It trains an encoder-decoder to reconstruct real faces, then uses the reconstruction error as a discriminative signal: real faces reconstruct faithfully while manipulated faces leave residuals in the error map. The encoder is an Xception backbone pretrained on ImageNet. At inference, encoder features are concatenated with the reconstruction error and passed to a binary classifier.

The core insight is that a network trained to reconstruct only real faces will struggle to reconstruct fakes; the residual difference is itself the discriminative feature. This makes RECCE agnostic to the specific manipulation method used. However, RECCE relies heavily on spatial and textural artifacts that are destroyed by heavy video compression, causing the large C23→C40 accuracy drop we aim to address.


RECCE architecture. The encoder-decoder reconstructs real faces; the reconstruction error combined with encoder features is used for binary classification.


Proposed Updates

We test three architectural modifications to RECCE, individually and in combination, to understand each one's contribution to closing the generalization gap.

Update #1: CBAM Attention Modules

Convolutional Block Attention Modules (CBAM) were inserted after Xception encoder blocks 1, 2, and 4. CBAM applies channel attention (which feature maps matter) followed by spatial attention (which spatial locations matter), guiding the encoder to focus on manipulation-relevant face regions rather than compression-induced texture patterns. This is the single most impactful modification: CBAM alone raises C40 accuracy from 74.00% to 80.48% and cuts the generalization gap from 0.2300 to 0.1652, a 28% reduction, while preserving full C23 accuracy (97.00%).


CBAM-only model on C40: accuracy improves to ~77%, a major improvement over the 74% baseline. (Gap = 0.1652)

Update #2: Frequency Branch (FFT Features)

A Frequency Branch was added alongside the spatial Xception encoder. It applies torch.fft.fft2 to the input image, extracts the log-magnitude spectrum, and processes it through a 4-layer CNN. A learned gate blends the frequency features into the spatial features. The motivation is that GAN-based deepfakes leave characteristic fingerprints in the frequency domain (checkerboard artifacts, spectral peaks) not easily captured by spatial convolutions. When used alone, the frequency branch offers minimal improvement on C40 (73.50% vs 74.00% baseline) because C40 compression destroys the high-frequency signals the branch relies on. However, when combined with CBAM and Multi-Task Head in the final configuration, it contributes to the overall best result of 81.60% C40 accuracy.


Frequency-only model on C40: marginal change versus baseline (73.50% vs 74.00%). Frequency features alone are fragile under heavy compression. (Gap = 0.2270)

Update #3: Multi-Task Heads

Two auxiliary output heads were added to enrich the shared encoder with additional supervisory signal: a manipulation-type classifier (predicting which of the four FF++ methods was used) and a binary mask predictor (localising the manipulated region). These heads force the encoder to learn more semantically meaningful features rather than relying on compression-sensitive texture cues. The Multi-Task Head alone reduces the gap to 0.1988 (C40 = 76.13%). Crucially, when paired with CBAM and the Frequency Branch, the multi-task supervision resolves the interference between the two other modules; the final combination achieves the best C40 accuracy of 81.60% and gap of 0.1540, a 33% reduction from baseline.


Best model (CBAM + Frequency + Multi-Task Head) on C40: accuracy reaches 81.60%. The matrix is nearly symmetric, showing balanced performance on both real and fake samples. (Gap = 0.1540)

Technical Report

Training and evaluation details for all six configurations:

  • Programming framework: PyTorch (DeepfakeBench codebase)
  • Training hardware: Personal laptop with NVIDIA GPU, 6 GB VRAM
  • Backbone: Xception pretrained on ImageNet
  • Optimizer: Adam, learning rate 2e-4, weight decay 1e-4
  • Batch size: 32
  • Frames per video: 16 (all configurations)
  • Number of epochs: 20 with early stopping on validation AUC
  • Training time per run: approximately 6 to 8 hours
  • CBAM placement: after Xception encoder blocks 1, 2, and 4
  • Frequency Branch: 4-layer CNN on log-magnitude FFT spectrum with learned gate blending
  • Multi-Task heads: manipulation-type classifier (4 classes: DF, F2F, FS, NT) + binary mask predictor
  • Key finding: CBAM is the dominant modification; it alone reduces the gap by 28%. The Frequency Branch on its own is limited by compression destroying high-frequency GAN fingerprints. The Multi-Task Head adds richer supervision that resolves the interference between CBAM and Frequency features, enabling the best combined result.

Conclusion

Our experiments show that architectural modifications can substantially close the compression generalization gap in deepfake detection, reducing it from 0.2300 to 0.1540 (a 33% improvement) without sacrificing C23 accuracy.

Key findings:

  • CBAM is the most impactful single modification. By directing the encoder toward manipulation-relevant spatial regions, CBAM alone raises C40 accuracy by over 6 points and cuts the gap to 0.1652. It works because attention-guided features are more semantically grounded and less dependent on compression-sensitive texture artifacts.
  • Frequency features alone are fragile under heavy compression. The FFT magnitude spectrum encodes GAN fingerprints that are entirely destroyed by C40 compression, providing no benefit on its own. However, it contributes positively as part of the full combination.
  • CBAM + Frequency alone is counterproductive. Adding frequency features to CBAM actually hurts C40 performance (74.01% vs 80.48%), suggesting that the two modules interfere without additional regularisation from the multi-task heads.
  • Multi-Task supervision resolves the interference. The auxiliary manipulation-type and mask prediction losses force the shared encoder to learn semantically richer representations, which stabilises training and enables all three modifications to work synergistically, yielding the best C40 accuracy of 81.60% and gap of 0.1540.

Future work: Applying domain adaptation (e.g. adversarial feature alignment between C23 and C40 distributions) could further reduce the gap. Testing on other compression codecs (H.264, H.265) and cross-dataset evaluation (trained on FF++, tested on Celeb-DF) would assess whether these gains hold in broader real-world conditions.
